Five Hundred Credits

About this Project

We are a team of Singapore University of Technology and Design (SUTD) students that seeks to explore the ethical issues in our society of the future, set in the year 2219. Firstly, in crafting our future society, we looked at the work of Alberto Vanolo whom we have discussed in class and the ideas that were displayed by artists in the Art Science Museum. In the Art Science Museum, the artists explored a multitude of scenarios, largely related to the environment and how society’s perception of life would change. As living conditions on the Earth’s surface were no longer suitable for humans, life started to migrate underground after having made significant progress to make the underground environment habitable. However, as not everything could be transported below, the idea of sky cities for the sustenance of animals and plants was born. In his work, Alberto explored the four imaginaries of smart cities questioning the position of citizens. However all four imaginaries locate citizens in a subaltern position as he feels that the smart cities vision has a large disconnect from societal needs and aspirations. In coming up with the subterranean society, we took inspiration from Alberto’s subjugated citizens in a dystopian city. In this scenario, citizens become subaltern subjects of the government as countries rely more heavily on technology to sustain and improve life in a harsher climate. Control and privacy are ceded to the government in exchange for integration into the society. On the other hand Aeronauts who are considered the upper class of society partake in active citizenship operating as urban sensors. The citizens in this category are more involved and responsible for taking care of the environment. They help to generate data through the wearable sensors that are incorporated into their daily lives.

Ethical Issues on the Social Credit System & “Big Brother” Surveillance
We have based our game on two key technologies: Social Credit System and “Big Brother” surveillance.

According to Rachel Botzman, in the era of a shared economy, a mature credit system plays an important role in society. Just like China’s Social Credit System, this tool is used in smart citizenship and can determine one’s rights and privileges. e.g. Greater number of credits = higher social standing. With a higher social class, a citizen can gain significant social privileges and benefits (as shown from the path towards obtaining a ministry position).

This evaluation system brings about both advantages and disadvantages to society. The Social Credit System is considered a “carrot and stick” approach, which is a traditional motivation theory, in motivating people to elicit good behavior. Both the idea of incentivizing positive behaviour and punishing negative behaviour is portrayed in our game. The evaluation of a person’s trustworthiness benefits those who are more honest and ultimately cultivates a more morally upright society. However, if the system is too rigid, the authenticity of behaviour is compromised and there is a need for citizens to put on a facade.

On top of that, one big disadvantage of the Social Credit System is the divergence from the posthuman ideal. It goes against the posthuman movement because the system encourages a divide within the society. Based on the amount of credits you have, you are classified as either an upper class or lower class citizen. This results in dualism, separating one group from another.

With regards to surveillance, the main issue is privacy concerns. In our game, the government misuses the smart technology (the contact lenses) to spy on their citizens and monitor their every move. In current day, there is already an example of such technology called “skynet” that uses artificial intelligence, facial recognition and big data analysis. While this current technology is used to tag and track “wanted individuals” through the use of security cameras, the technology may be significantly improved and implemented in smart objects such as contact lenses to track any citizen’s behaviour. After which, the government collect a citizen’s personal data, potentially resulting in privacy infringement without their knowledge.

In the scenario where the player decides to accept or reject the ministry job, it brings about the questions: “Must I really try my best to be a “ideal” citizen to get more credits?” and “At what level must I reach till I am satisfied with my goal in life?”
